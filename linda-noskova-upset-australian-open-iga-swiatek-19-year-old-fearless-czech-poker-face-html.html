It wasn't supposed to be this way - but as a number of matches across this year's women singles draw have shown, the 2024 Australian Open has no plans to stick to a script. 

Since the tournament kicked off at the start of last week, upsets have been rife as seed after seed fell to unfancied opponents. But many thought Iga Swiatek would be bullet-proof. 

On a peerless match-winning run, the Polish superstar - and world No 1 - looked almost assured of meeting last year's winner Aryna Sabalenka in a classic final at Rod Laver Arena. 


But fate had other plans and instead, the French Open champion lost her first match in her last 19: 6-3, 3-6, 4-6 to Czech teenager Linda Noskova. 

For those who have been following the 19-year-old's budding career, watching Noskova take yet another high-profile scalp might have come as little surprise. But very few indeed have been watching - until Saturday's showstopping win forced her into the spotlight. 

Linda Noskova pulled off the upset of the tournament with her elimination of Iga Swiatek

The Polish world No 1 (right) was widely expected to face Aryna Sabalenka in Melbourne final

The 19-year-old up-and-comer has been breaking records and collecting top-seed scalps en route to the Australian Open quarter-finals

After a distressing walkover against Ukraine's Elina Svitolina - who pulled up after just three games on Monday - Noskova will play her first grand slam quarter-final against fellow unseeded surprise contender Dayana Yastremka on Tuesday. Before this tournament, Noskova had only made it as far as the second round. 

But where did the talented teen, currently ranked No 50, come from? And how has she come off against top-ranked opponents before? 

Here, Mail Sport looks into the stratospheric rise of the Australian Open title contender, who only has one seed left on her side of the draw before the prospect of a major final.   

Top of the class 

While 16-year-old Russian prodigy Mirra Andreeva has enjoyed the lion's share of the headlines for her blockbuster, ahead-of-her-time, performances at grand slams, Noskova has been quietly making headway in the women's game since breaking into the top 100 in 2022. 

Doing so in August 2022, Noskova became the youngest WTA player inside 100 at just 17, spurred on by her first-ever semi-final appearance on the circuit at the Makarska Open. 

Noskova only had to wait a year until she became the youngest player inside the top 50, and netted a career-high ranking of 40 later on in 2023. 

Andreeva may have pipped her to the post as the WTA's Most Improved Player last season after both teens were nominated, but Noskova has already gone one better in Melbourne after the Krasnoyarsk teen was knocked out by her compatriot Barbora Krejcikova. 

Victory in Melbourne would be by far the Czech's biggest prize - up to this point, the Vsetin-born player's biggest title is the 2022 Reinert Open, an ITF 100,000 event. 

Although untried as a senior player, Noskova tasted grand slam success at junior level in 2021 when she swept through the competition at Roland Garros to become French Open Girls champion as an unseeded player. 

Noskova had turned professional two years early in July 2019, after first picking up the sport at the age of six on the recommendation of her father, Drahos Nosk.  

In 2021, Noskova won the French Open Junior singles title as an unseeded player in Paris

Less than three years later, a walkover from Elina Svitolina (prone) has allowed Noskova to break new grand slam ground

A reputation for giant-killings

The player has already enjoyed a blistering run in Australia, forcing a number of top-ranked players into early retirement at the 2023 Adelaide Open. 

Former world No 1 Victoria Azarenka was knocked out of competition by the unseeded Czech after taking her Belarusian opponent the distance 6-4, 6-7, 7-6 (8-6).

But it was in the semi-finals where Noskova was able to show off her full potential against big beast opponents, beating two-time Wimbledon finalist Ons Jabeur. 

The Tunisian phenom was the tournament's No 1 seeded player, but struggled at times with injury, allowing the qualifier to pounce and close out a 6-3, 1-6, 6-3 victory Down Under.   

Noskova was eventually stopped in her tracks in the finals by Sabalenka, but she put the fear of God in her opponent, then in the thick of her own miraculous Australian swing run. 

'I mean, you can feel that she isn't really afraid of us,' Sabalenka said of Noskova's approach to her fellow seeds. 'She just plays her game. No matter what, she just plays her game.

'I didn't feel today that she's getting tight and she's going to give me easy points. I felt like I have to win this match. That impressed me a lot.'

Nor did she leave without learning a thing or two, as Swiatek attested after their match over the weekend.

The Czech connection 

In her box in Melbourne sits Tomas Krupa, a compatriot of Noskova's with an eye-catching pedigree. 

A former doubles player himself, Krupa has worked with Jiri Vesely, Strycova, Karolina Pliskova, Radek Stepanek, and helped guide Tomas Berdych to his 2010 Wimbledon final appearance. 

Krupa is a relatively recent hire, having been first consulted over working with the teenager in December 2022 by David Kotyza, who also manages the player. 

Noskova works with her compatriot Tomas Krupa, who previously coached Tomas Berdych to a Wimbledon singles final

Brand Noskova 

As is so often the case, with young talent comes a raft of early sponsorship deals. 

As well as sporting Yonex as her kit and racquet sponsor, in October 2022 less than a month before her 18th birthday, Noskova signed up with Swiss watchmaker Rado. 

During her turn at the US Open, Noskova partnered with Sonesta International Hotels, publicising her stay at New York's The Fifty in Midtown Manhattan on her Instagram. 

But the stay was a brief one; Jabeur took her revenge against the upstart in their second-round meeting, beating Noskova 7-6, 4-6, 6-3 in Flushing Meadows. 

As well as clothing sponsorships, Noskova is also an ambassador for Royaltiz, a proprietary trading platform that allows fans to 'invest' in sports talents - including tennis players - using purchasable tokens or NFTs (non-fungible tokens). She's also sponsored by software development company OKsystem, whose branding she sports on her kit. 

Despite her age, Noskova has a raft of brand deals including partnership with Swiss label Rado

Off the court, Noskova has been described by Krupa as a quiet presence who takes naps between tournament games

Tearing down her idols 

For all of the right-hander's comparisons to Slavic talents like Rybakina and Sabalenka, like so many of her peers, Noskova's number one role model is All-American.  

'Serena Williams is my biggest idol from the very beginning, so I'm trying to go in her path,' Noskova said in 2023, before doubling-down on the statement after moving past Svitolina on Monday. 

'I was a big fan of Serena,' Noskova said of her formative models. 'I always am and always probably will be. 

'I like her style a lot and the way she carries herself and the person that she is. She's definitely on top of my charts.'

But the player also admitted that she has become a big fan of Wimbledon semi-finalist Svitolina too, adding: 'I like her style a lot. She can change the direction or the rhythm of the game really good, so I was always really appreciated of such a player that can go on the net or can do slice.'

While Svitolina had little choice but to concede due to her back issue, both the Ukrainian and Williams have received similar treatment from a young player with little sympathy for heroines on court.

The teenager described the moment as 'her favourite' as a long-term fan of iconic champion

Partnered with compatriot Lucie Hradecka, Noskova blew past the family doubles pairing of Williams and her sister Venus in the first round of the 2022 US Open, later describing it as her 'favourite moment' in the sport on social media. 

Noskova saved similar treatment for a homegrown icons, Petra Kvitova - who she beat in Cincinnati in 2023 

Silence is golden 

In an interview in 2023, Krupa claimed Noskova's off-court default mode is one of silence and reflection and that between tournament fixtures she has a particular capacity for catching up on sleep. 

This no doubt has an impact on her composure on-court, which is admirable. 

When asked about her cool demeanour and poker face on show against Swiatek, Noskova stressed that it was all 'just part of (her)'. 

Noskova frequently shares pictures of her companion back home - her cat named Mitch

'I'm just really happy to be like that,' Noskova said. 'Not really emotional even after wins or losses. That's great.

'But sometimes people are like, "Does it mean anything to you?" I'm like, "Yeah, it does, but I'm just not showing it."'

Less quiet, however, is her music taste - Noskova is thought to be a fan of Lizzo, Lil Naz X, and Italian Eurovision rockers Maneskin. 

Little is known about Noskova's personal life, save for sharing it with one animal in particular - her cat named Mitch.
