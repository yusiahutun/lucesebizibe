Bottega Veneta Spring 2024 Takes Us on a Journey

August 2024 · 5 minute read

On the second night of Milan Fashion Week, I was discussing the day’s runway shows with two colleagues over pasta, wine, and veal Milanese. We talked about brand identity and the evolution of design, as well as, of course, the clothes, the bags, and the shoes—what we loved, what we liked, and what could have done without. Then, with full bellies and jet-lagged minds, we flipped the switch on fashion and turned to the importance of travel. Not about specific destinations, but rather the journeys we'd taken that had impacted each of us, the trips that helped us make life-altering decisions, feel closer to nature, or restore our faith, in people, in the world, in a higher power.

Perhaps it wasn't just the time zone-change that made us think of wanderlust, but rather all the clues that Bottega Veneta’s creative director Matthieu Blazy had left leading up to his runway show at the end of the week. The first were the brand's livestream ads, which featured spinning globes and outlines of maps. The second was the show invite, which included a compass fastened to a leather watch strap. Entering the runway space, we were met with another hint: the floors and parts of the interior façade were painted with fish, birds, shells, and what appeared to be topography of land and water (the art of which, Blazy said later, was inspired by the back cover of a map he had as a kid). Since becoming creative director in 2021, Blazy has spent the last three seasons honing in on the Italian DNA of Bottega Veneta, keeping his focus to reinterpreting and reviving house codes and craft. So where in the world was he going to take us now?

It turns out the answer was everywhere and anywhere. As Blazy noted backstage after the show, he “started with the idea of a journey, so as the model was walking down the runway it was this idea of their own journey, put together with our journey.” It was about “blending worlds” and “pulling inspiration literally from all over the world: South America, South East Asia, Russia, Brittany, Sicily. Everywhere was a new possibility.” And indeed, the collection had a suitcase packed full of promise and hope and curiosity and wonder.

Courtesy of Bottega Veneta

You felt it in the organic curves and shapes of his ready-to-wear. Particularly enchanting were the raffia pompoms attached to dresses that almost resembled a wearable, Seussian moss patch, one that had been joyfully rolled around in. Some necklines were cut in such a way that it looked like they were almost in the process of melting, as if the the model, or “character” as Blazy called them, had gotten stuck in some exotic forest during rainy season or, their clothes were flung open from a wind gusts. Blazy also said that nothing in the collection was embroidered, all of the twisting and knotting and fringing were formed and fitted by hand. (Backstage after the show, Blazy said he challenged his atelier to try new ways of doing things with this collection, and that the team had fun.) Compelling, too, were the more down-to-earth pieces, clean, precise suiting and separates in plain blacks and whites that anchored a collection interspersed with so many spectacular, couture-level pieces.

The bags also, in keeping with the heritage of the fashion house, were spectacularly crafted, particularly the giant garden tote made with strips of woven leather that resembled real banana leaf. Other carryalls were exactly that: giant bags, some sacks and some cases, that you wanted to grab and throw over your shoulder and get moving to somewhere you’ve never been before.

The soundtrack included artists that evoked the idea of travel, like Fleetwood Mac, but also Andrea Bocelli, highlighting the very Italian-ness of the collection. That touch of local romance also served as a reminder that Blazy was successfully evolving the codes of Bottega Veneta with such grace and intelligence, so as not to give us regurgitations of the past but to transform it. This notion of how journeys, just like clothes and art, can help to transform us too was the real crux of the collection. To know that there were so many people watching his transformative work come down the runway, whether they were those watching the livestream remotely, or they were in the audience—like Erykah Badu, Shelly-Ann Fraser-Pryce, and Gucci’s new head of creative Sabato De Sarno among them—felt optimistic.

Courtesy of Bottega Veneta

Courtesy of Bottega Veneta

Maybe it made them think about their own trajectories, their own desires to be more intrepid. The workmanship, the clothes, and the curious characters wearing them felt like a sort of connective tissue between us all and for a few minutes we were able to think outside the confines of our Instagram feeds, outside of the walls of the runway space, even outside of Milan. Like at my dinner a few nights earlier, the idea of travel gave us a jolt to get out, to see where we can really go, to find newness and inspiration. And if we get to wear something from Blazy while we do that, how lucky we will be.
